Here’s a detailed summary of the article titled *“Misi PELARI SOLO Negara Tawan 10 Menara Malaysia & 30 Menara Tertinggi Dunia (MM1030)”* on the site Nona Magazine. ([Nona][1])

---

### 1. Who is Dr Suwaibah?

* Dr Suwaibah Muhammad Nasir is a solo runner from Malaysia, previously a member of the Royal Malaysian Air Force (TUDM). ([Nona][1])
* Among her notable past feats: she became the first Malaysian woman to run solo across the Sahara Desert, covering 1,111 km from Aswan to Port Said in Egypt over 32 days. ([airforce.mil.my][2])
* She holds multiple records in the Malaysia Book of Records. ([airforce.mil.my][2])

---

### 2. What is the mission MM1030?

* The mission is called **MM1030** (short for *Misi Menawan 10 Menara Malaysia & 30 Menara Tertinggi Dunia*). ([Nona][1])
* It aims for two major goals:

  1. Conquer (run/dedicate) at least **10 towers in Malaysia**. ([Sinar Harian][3])
  2. Conquer **30 of the highest towers in the world** via solo runs. ([MYNEWSHUB][4])

---

### 3. What are the Malaysian towers?

* For the Malaysian component (10 towers), some of the named ones are:

  * Menara Rebung TM
  * Menara Alor Setar
  * Menara Komtar
  * Menara Kuantan 188
    ([Sinar Harian][3])
* The mission would start in July of the year of article and was expected to finish by end of that same year. ([Sinar Harian][3])

---

### 4. International component: 30 towers worldwide

* The mission to “30 menara tertinggi dunia” involves visiting (running/up the stairs of) iconic tall towers around the world. For example:

  * She had already conquered the Avala Tower in Belgrade, Serbia (height ~250 m) on 31 August 2016. ([MYNEWSHUB][4])
  * She also climbed the Baiyoke Sky Tower in Thailand, 309 m tall, recording a time of 14 min 17 sec. ([MYNEWSHUB][4])
* The mission was tied to a humanitarian theme: promoting peace, and being symbolic of “1 World 1 Hope”. ([airforce.mil.my][2])

---

### 5. Why is she doing this?

* One major motivation: to **promote the sport of “stairs scaling” (vertical running/pushing up towers)** among Malaysian youth. ([Sinar Harian][3])
* She plans to establish a training academy in Malaysia to nurture young talent for this sport, preparing them to compete internationally—e.g., at the Southeast Asian Games, Commonwealth Games, even potentially the Olympic Games. ([Sinar Harian][3])
* She calls on government agencies, NGOs, corporate sponsors to support the initiative. ([Nona][1])

---

### 6. Financial & logistic challenges

* For the global “30 towers” mission, the cost is significant. She estimated cost of about **US$1 million (~RM4 million)** covering flights, visas, accommodation, and running gear for travelling to multiple countries. ([Utusan Malaysia][5])
* A press statement from the Malaysian Ministry of Defence clarified that although they support her efforts, they are not sponsoring the full cost. 

---

### 7. Launch & Patronage

* The launch of the MM1030 mission was officiated by the Tengku Hassanal Ibrahim Alam Shah (Pemangku Raja Pahang) who is the Royal Patron (Penaung Diraja) of MM1030. ([Nona][1])

---

### 8. Significance & impact

* The mission helps to put Malaysia on the map in the niche sport of vertical tower running.
* It promotes physical fitness, adventure sport, youth development.
* It carries a humanitarian/peace message via the global runs.
* The training academy idea helps build a pipeline of future athletes in this sport in Malaysia.

---

### 9. Summary of key milestones

* Solo Sahara crossing: 1,111 km in Egypt.
* Launch of MM1030 (10 Malaysian towers + 30 global towers).
* Named towers in Malaysia to be climbed starting from July.
* Example global tower climbs: Avala Tower (Serbia), Baiyoke Sky Tower (Thailand).
* Financial target ~RM4 million for mission.
* Academy planned for Malaysian youth.

---

### 10. Conclusion

In essence, Dr Suwaibah’s mission is **bold and multifaceted**: combining extreme sport (running up tall towers), international adventure, national pride, youth development, and humanitarian themes. Her drive to scale Malaysian and global towers underscores both personal endurance and a push to popularise “stairs-scaling” as a legitimate sport in Malaysia.
